# Learning Review: Payment Service Double-Charge During Infrastructure Failover **Date**: 2026-03-05 **Reviewed**: 2026-03-26 **Severity**: High — 312 customers double-charged, £28,400 financial exposure **Duration**: ~44 minutes from failover start to detection (4 min failover + 40 min until customer complaint) **Resolution**: Refunds issued within 6 hours of detection --- ## 1. Situation Snapshot During a scheduled infrastructure failover on 2026-03-05, the payment processing service double-charged 312 customers. The root mechanism was a counter-reset fallback in the idempotency key generation: when the counter service was unreachable during failover, the payment service reset its attempt counter to 1, producing idempotency keys identical in structure but not actually duplicates of prior keys — allowing Stripe to process what were effectively duplicate charges as new, legitimate transactions. The incident was detected by a customer complaint, not by internal monitoring. Total financial exposure was £28,400; all refunds were completed within 6 hours. --- ## 2. Contributing Factors Each of the following conditions was independently necessary for this outcome. Remove any single one, and the double-charges do not occur. **Factor 1: The counter-reset fallback produced structurally valid but semantically wrong idempotency keys.** The fallback was designed to keep the payment service functional when the counter service was unavailable. Resetting attempt_number to 1 produced keys that were syntactically correct (order_id + attempt_number) but semantically incorrect — they matched the format of a first attempt, not a retry. This made the duplicate charge invisible to every downstream system. The fallback was a reasonable defensive design choice at the time: keeping payments flowing during a brief counter-service blip is a sensible priority. The failure mode — that the reset could produce key collisions during a longer outage window with real traffic — was a second-order effect that was not obvious from the local context of the fallback logic. **Factor 2: The failover runbook did not document the payment service's dependency on the counter service for idempotency correctness.** The counter service was classified as non-critical and restarted last. This classification was accurate for most of its functions but missed the fact that it was load-bearing for payment correctness. Kobe had updated the runbook 3 months prior for a different dependency, demonstrating that the team had the practice of maintaining dependency documentation — this particular dependency simply had not been identified yet. **Factor 3: The idempotency key design assumed continuous counter service availability.** MJ's architecture used the counter service as a single source of truth for attempt numbering. This was a clean design for the normal case. The assumption of availability was implicit — not documented as a constraint — which meant the fallback could be added without recognizing it was violating a foundational invariant. **Factor 4: Stripe's 24-hour deduplication window did not catch the duplicates.** Stripe deduplicates on idempotency keys within a 24-hour window. If the reset had produced a key that had already been submitted, Stripe would have returned the original charge. But because the counter reset to 1 for a *new* set of transactions (not retries of earlier ones), the keys were genuinely novel from Stripe's perspective. The external safety net existed but did not cover this failure mode. **Factor 5: No monitoring existed for idempotency key uniqueness or duplicate charge detection.** The team monitored charge success/failure rates, which remained normal throughout the incident. There was no alert on duplicate charges per order, duplicate idempotency keys, or counter-service-unavailable-while-payments-are-processing. The system was operating in a degraded state that was invisible to all existing observability. **Factor 6: Acceptance criteria for the payment service did not cover counter-service-unavailable scenarios.** Bird's domain rule — a customer must never be charged more than once for a single order — was clear. But the acceptance criteria that encoded this rule did not include a test for the degraded-counter scenario. The gap between the domain rule and its technical enforcement was not visible at the acceptance-criteria level. **Factor 7: The fallback path was never tested against a full infrastructure failover.** Shaq's fallback was implemented 8 months prior. It was likely tested against transient counter-service unavailability (a brief blip) but not against the scenario where real payment traffic flows while the counter service is down for minutes. The difference matters: a brief blip with no traffic is benign; minutes of real traffic with reset counters produces collisions at scale. --- ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that idempotency key generation is a domain-critical invariant, not just a technical convenience.** This means fallback behavior for any component involved in idempotency must be reviewed against the domain rule ("never double-charge"), not just against the technical contract ("produce a valid key"). - **We now know that "non-critical" service classification can be context-dependent.** The counter service is non-critical for most operations but critical for payment correctness. This means service criticality should be assessed per-dependency, not as a blanket classification. - **We now know that our monitoring has a blind spot for "successful but wrong" states.** Charge success rates looked healthy throughout the incident. This means we need monitoring that validates business correctness (one charge per order), not just technical success (charge processed). - **We now know that fallback paths that silently degrade correctness guarantees are more dangerous than fallback paths that fail loudly.** A payment service that returned errors during the failover would have been noticed immediately. The silent degradation delayed detection by 40 minutes. This means fallback designs should prefer noisy failure over silent incorrectness for domain-critical operations. - **We now know that acceptance criteria must encode degraded-mode behavior for domain-critical rules.** The domain rule existed; the happy-path test existed; the degraded-mode test did not. This means any domain rule tagged as "must never be violated" needs acceptance criteria that cover known fallback/degraded paths. --- ## 5.
