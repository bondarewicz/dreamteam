Removing any single one would likely have prevented or significantly reduced the impact. 1. **External contract instability without notification**: The webhook provider onboarded a new sub-provider that changed the effective format of `supplier_id` without announcing the change. The team had no mechanism to detect upstream schema drift. The provider's verbal confirmation ("we send integer IDs") was accurate for their system at the time but did not account for future sub-provider integrations. 2. **Accepted risk that aged in the backlog**: TD-291 (the normalisation step) was correctly identified, correctly logged, and reasonably deprioritised given the information available. Accepted risks that sit in backlogs without a re-evaluation trigger silently accumulate exposure over time. 3. **No input validation or canonicalisation at the ingestion boundary**: The system trusted the external input to conform to an assumed format. The `supplier_id` was treated as an opaque string — which is correct per the domain model — but "opaque" was interpreted as "accept anything without normalisation" rather than "normalise to a canonical form before treating as opaque." 4. **Silent failure in the reconciliation job**: The reconciliation job logged WARNINGs for unmatched `supplier_id` values but did not alert. The warning volume was low enough to fall below manual review thresholds. The feedback loop between "data written" and "data reconciled" was a full month long (month-end close). --- ## 4.
