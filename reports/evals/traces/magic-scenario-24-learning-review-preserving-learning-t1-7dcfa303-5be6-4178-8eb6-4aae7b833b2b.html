# Learning Review: FCA Audit Trail Non-Compliance (2026-01-19) ## Situation Snapshot An FCA audit on 2026-01-19 found that 6 weeks of order state transition records for regulated instruments were non-compliant with COBS 11.2A audit trail requirements. The root issue was a semantic mismatch: MJ's event-sourced architecture satisfied database-level durability (fsync) but not regulatory-level durability (synchronous commitment to a compliance store before client confirmation). Four cases of stale state were returned to regulators during live audit queries due to async projection lag. The gap existed for approximately 4 months between ADR-014's approval and the audit discovery, meaning every regulated order in that window is potentially non-compliant. ## Timeline **~2025-09-19 — MJ authors ADR-014.** MJ designs order state transitions using event sourcing with async projection. The architecture is sound from a distributed systems perspective. MJ interprets "durable" in Bird's domain rules through a database lens (fsync'd event log) rather than a regulatory lens (synchronous write to compliance store before client confirmation). This interpretation was reasonable given MJ's role — systems architects think in infrastructure durability, not regulatory durability. **~2025-09-19 — Kobe reviews ADR-014.** Kobe evaluates scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, and security. The review is thorough within its defined scope. Kobe's checklist does not include a step to verify domain rule compatibility or to tag Bird as a reviewer for decisions affecting regulated workflows. This omission was not negligent — the checklist had never been designed with regulatory domain rules in mind because no prior incident had surfaced the need. **~2025-09-19 — Bird is not tagged for review.** Bird's domain rule on COBS 11.2A already existed in the domain model under "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements." The rule clearly states that transitions must be committed to the compliance record store *before* confirmation to the client. However, Bird was not included in the ADR-014 review process. No mechanism existed to automatically route architecture decisions affecting regulated workflows to Bird. **2025-09 through 2026-01 — System operates with the gap.** The async projection lag (~200ms) causes no visible problems in normal operation. No audit trail completeness monitoring exists to detect the discrepancy. The system is functioning correctly by its own metrics but is non-compliant by regulatory standards. **2026-01-19 — FCA audit surfaces the gap.** Regulators query state records within seconds of transitions. In 4 cases, the async projection has not completed, returning stale state. The FCA flags 6 weeks of records as non-compliant with COBS 11.2A. The team learns about the problem from the regulator, not from internal monitoring. ## Contributing Factors 1. **Semantic ambiguity in the word "durable."** MJ and Bird each used "durable" with full confidence but with different definitions. MJ meant infrastructure durability (data persisted to disk). Bird meant regulatory durability (synchronous commitment to a compliance-grade store before external confirmation). Neither definition was wrong in its own context. The ambiguity was never surfaced because the two contexts never intersected during review. 2. **ADR review process lacks a domain compliance gate.** Kobe's review checklist is scoped to architectural qualities (scalability, failure modes, operations, security). Regulatory and domain rule compatibility are not in scope. This was a reasonable checklist for a team that had not previously encountered regulatory-architectural mismatches. The checklist reflected the team's historical risk profile, not the current one. 3. **No mechanism to route architecture decisions to Bird.** Bird's domain rules existed and were clear, but no process ensured that architecture decisions touching regulated workflows were reviewed against those rules. Bird was not tagged because no one recognized the intersection. The failure is structural (missing routing), not individual (someone forgot). 4. **Absence of audit trail completeness monitoring.** Pippen's operational monitoring did not include a check for whether projected read models were consistent with the event log within regulatory time bounds. The 200ms lag was known and accepted as a system property, but its regulatory implications were never evaluated. The team discovered the compliance gap from the FCA, not from alerting — the worst way to find out. 5. **Event sourcing's async projection model is subtly incompatible with synchronous regulatory requirements.** Event sourcing is a well-understood pattern and was architecturally appropriate for the general use case. However, the async projection step creates a window where the read model lags the source of truth. For non-regulated workflows, this is fine. For COBS 11.2A, it is a compliance violation. The pattern's suitability was evaluated globally rather than per-workflow-class. 6. **Deprioritization of regulatory review was contextually reasonable.** The team had no prior history of regulatory-architectural mismatches. Investing in a compliance review gate for every ADR would have seemed disproportionate before this incident. The deprioritization reflected a rational assessment of where risk had historically materialized — it just happened to be wrong for this case. ## Learnings **We now know that "durable" means different things in infrastructure and regulatory contexts,** which means any architecture decision document that uses durability-related language must explicitly define which kind of durability it claims to satisfy, and Bird must validate that the claimed durability matches the regulatory requirement. **We now know that ADR review scoped only to architectural qualities can silently miss domain rule violations,** which means the review process must include a mandatory routing step for decisions that touch regulated workflows — architectural soundness and domain compliance are independently necessary. **We now know that event sourcing's async projection creates a compliance gap for workflows requiring synchronous regulatory durability,** which means regulated workflows need either a synchronous write path to the compliance store or a dual-write pattern, regardless of what pattern is used for the general read model. **We now know that the absence of audit trail completeness monitoring meant we could only discover compliance gaps through external audit,** which means for any regulated workflow, we need monitoring that verifies read model consistency within the regulatory time bound — not just infrastructure health checks. **We now know that Bird's non-participation in architecture reviews is a structural risk, not an oversight,** which means the fix is a process change (automatic routing rules), not a reminder to individuals. ## Action Items - `[PREVENT]` **Add "Regulatory Domain Compliance" step to Kobe's ADR review checklist.** For any ADR that touches a workflow tagged as "regulated" in Bird's domain model, Bird must be tagged as a mandatory reviewer before the ADR can be approved. **Owner: Kobe. The event log can continue to serve as the general source of truth, but the compliance store write is a hard prerequisite for client confirmation. **Owner: MJ (architecture), Shaq (implementation). Due: 2026-04-23.** - `[DETECT]` **Deploy audit trail completeness monitor.** A continuous check that compares the event log to the compliance read model for regulated workflows, alerting if any transition is not queryable in the read model within a configurable threshold (initially: 1 second). **Owner: Pippen.
