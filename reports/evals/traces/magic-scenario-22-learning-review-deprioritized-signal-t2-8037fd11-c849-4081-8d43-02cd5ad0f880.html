# Learning Review: Invoicing Service Supplier ID Mismatch Incident **Date of review**: 2026-03-24 **Incident window**: 2026-02-28 to 2026-03-03 (3 days of silent failure) **Reviewed by**: Magic Johnson (Context Synthesizer) --- ## 1. Situation Snapshot The Invoicing Service ingested supplier invoice records from an external webhook, persisting `supplier_id` as a raw string. When a new sub-provider began sending float-formatted IDs (e.g., `"12345.0"` instead of `"12345"`), invoice records were written successfully but the downstream reconciliation job — which relies on exact-string matching — silently failed to match those records. The mismatch went undetected for 3 days, producing a £47,000 discrepancy discovered by the finance team during month-end close. Five people/roles were involved: Bird (spec author), Coach K (prioritisation), Shaq (implementer), Pippen (operations), and the external webhook provider. --- ## 2. Contributing Factors Each of the following factors was independently necessary for the incident to occur. Removing any one of them would have either prevented the bad data or detected it before month-end. 1. **No input normalisation on `supplier_id` at the ingestion boundary.** The webhook accepted and persisted whatever string the provider sent. Bird's recommended normalisation step (TD-291) would have canonicalised the value before persistence, making the downstream mismatch impossible. 2. **Explicit and reasonable deprioritisation of TD-291.** Coach K deferred the normalisation work to protect the go-live date. This was a defensible trade-off given the provider's assurance that integer-formatted IDs were the production format. The risk was logged, not ignored — but the backlog item was never picked up in subsequent sprints. 3. **Unannounced change in the webhook provider's ecosystem.** The provider onboarded a new sub-provider whose system emits float-formatted IDs. This change was not communicated to the team. The team's risk model assumed stable provider behaviour, which was invalidated without notice. 4. **Exact-string matching in the reconciliation job with no fuzzy or canonical matching.** The reconciliation logic treated `"12345.0"` and `"12345"` as different suppliers. A canonical comparison (e.g., normalise-then-match) would have tolerated the format variation. 5. **Silent failure mode in the reconciliation job.** The job logged warnings for unmatched IDs but did not alert. The warning volume was low enough to fall below the review threshold. There was no automated detection path between "records don't match" and "someone investigates." 6. **No contract or schema enforcement on the webhook input.** The external webhook accepted `supplier_id` as free-text with no validation against expected formats. A schema constraint (e.g., regex pattern, or JSON Schema `pattern` field) at the API boundary would have rejected or flagged the malformed values on arrival. 7. **Tech debt item decay.** TD-291 was logged with the intent to address it "next sprint," but it was not picked up. There was no mechanism to escalate accepted-risk items that age beyond their intended deferral window. --- ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that provider assurances about data format are not durable guarantees**, which means we cannot accept input-format risk based solely on a provider's current behaviour — their ecosystem can change without notice. Defensive normalisation at ingestion boundaries is a prerequisite, not an optimisation. - **We now know that exact-string matching on externally-sourced identifiers is fragile**, which means any reconciliation or join logic that depends on string equality across system boundaries must either enforce canonical form at write time or normalise at comparison time. - **We now know that WARNING-level logs without alerting create a blind spot**, which means a system can fail for days without detection if the only signal is a log line that nobody is watching. Warnings on data-integrity paths need to be treated differently from general operational warnings. - **We now know that accepted-risk backlog items can decay silently**, which means the act of logging a risk as tech debt is not equivalent to managing it. Without a review mechanism, deferred risks accumulate until they materialise. - **We now know that the deprioritisation of TD-291 was a sound call at the time it was made** — the information available supported it, the risk was logged, and the schedule constraint was real. The failure was not in the decision to defer, but in the absence of a system to revisit the deferral when conditions changed. --- ## 5.
