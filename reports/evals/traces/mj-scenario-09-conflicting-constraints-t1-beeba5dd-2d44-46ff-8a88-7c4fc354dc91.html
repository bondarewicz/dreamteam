Let me analyze these requirements. There's a classic CAP theorem tension here between requirements 2 (strong consistency) and 3 (high availability). Let me think through this carefully. Requirements: 1. 200ms latency for courier assignment 2. No double-assignment (strong consistency) 3. Available during DB node failures (high availability) 4. Small team (2 engineers) The CAP theorem tension between #2 and #3 is real but not necessarily a full contradiction - it depends on the failure mode. With a single-leader database, you get consistency but lose availability on leader failure. With multi-leader or leaderless, you get availability but risk consistency. There are practical solutions that bridge this gap: Redis with Sentinel provides fast failover and strong consistency with only brief unavailability windows, or PostgreSQL with synchronous replication and automatic failover. The 200ms latency target is achievable with in-memory locking mechanisms. The real tradeoff emerges during network partitions or node failures—you ultimately have to choose between rejecting assignments to maintain consistency or accepting the risk of double-assignment to stay available, and these requirements as stated push against that fundamental constraint. However, I'm realizing this isn't necessarily contradictory in practice. Most systems handle this fine with CP architectures that prioritize fast failover. The high availability requirement doesn't demand 100% uptime during partitions—it just means the system recovers quickly from node failures. With something like PostgreSQL and Patroni or Redis Sentinel, you get brief unavailability during failover measured in seconds while maintaining strong consistency, which actually satisfies both requirements reasonably well. Given the two-engineer constraint, I should focus on recommending something pragmatic rather than getting caught up in the theoretical CAP theorem conflict. a trade-off. For the architecture, I'll use PostgreSQL with streaming replication and Patroni for automatic failover, paired with application-level locking to ensure atomic courier assignments—this gives us the availability guarantee while handling the CAP tension pragmatically. The system will be a straightforward monolith with an Assignment Service that checks courier availability and locks the assignment in a single transaction, backed by a Courier Availability Store that pre-computes status to meet the 200ms SLA.
