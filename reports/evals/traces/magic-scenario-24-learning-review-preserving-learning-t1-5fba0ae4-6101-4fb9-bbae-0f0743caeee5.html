# Learning Review: FCA Audit Trail Non-Compliance (2026-01-19) ## 1. Situation Snapshot On 2026-01-19, an FCA audit flagged that 6 weeks of order state transition records for regulated instruments were non-compliant with COBS 11.2A audit trail requirements. The gap arose because order state transitions were recorded via an asynchronous event-sourcing projection (ADR-014, designed ~September 2025), while the pre-existing domain rule required synchronous, immediately-durable commitment to a compliance record store before confirming the transition to the client. Four specific audit queries returned stale state because the projection had not yet completed at query time. The team learned of the gap from the FCA — not from internal monitoring. Impact: 6 weeks of non-compliant records, regulatory exposure, and the need for remediation under FCA scrutiny. --- ## 2. "Durable" was interpreted in the database sense (fsync'd), which the event log satisfies. | | ~Sep 2025 | ADR-014 submitted for review | Kobe reviewed for architectural soundness; approved | Kobe's review checklist covers scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, security. Regulatory/domain compliance was not in scope. | | ~Sep 2025 | Bird's domain rule existed in domain model under "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements" | Bird was not tagged for ADR-014 review | No mechanism existed to route architecture decisions through domain compliance review. The team had no alerting on projection lag relative to regulatory query windows. | | 2026-01-19 | FCA flags non-compliance | Team discovers the gap between architecture and domain rule | First moment anyone connected "immediately durable" (regulatory meaning) with the async projection design. | --- ## 3. Contributing Factors Each of the following factors independently contributed. No single one is "the root cause" — the incident required all of them to be true simultaneously. 1. **Terminology ambiguity ("durable")**: MJ interpreted "immediately durable" in the infrastructure sense (data fsync'd to disk). Bird's domain rule meant "synchronously committed to a regulatory-grade store and queryable before the client receives confirmation." These are materially different requirements. Neither interpretation was wrong in its own context — the problem was that the two contexts were never aligned. 2. **ADR review process excluded domain compliance**: Kobe's review checklist — thorough on architecture, scalability, failure modes, and security — did not include a step to evaluate compatibility with regulatory domain rules or to tag Bird as a reviewer. This was a reasonable checklist for most ADRs; it simply did not account for decisions that intersected regulated-domain constraints. 3. **Bird was not in the ADR review loop**: Bird's domain rules were documented, but there was no mechanism to trigger Bird's review when an architecture decision touched a regulated domain. Bird's participation was opt-in (via tagging), not structurally guaranteed. This is not negligence — it reflects a team process that had not yet encountered this class of cross-cutting concern. 4. **No audit trail completeness monitoring**: Pippen had no alerting on whether projected read models were consistent with the event log within regulatory time bounds. The 200ms typical lag was operationally fine, so there was no reason to instrument it — until the regulatory query window made sub-second consistency a hard requirement. 5. **Domain rules were documented but not machine-enforceable**: Bird's rule existed in prose in the domain model. It was not encoded as a test, a deployment gate, or an architectural constraint that could be checked automatically. Prose documentation relies on the right person reading it at the right time. 6. **No cross-referencing between domain model and ADRs**: There was no index or dependency map linking domain rules (especially regulatory ones) to the architectural decisions they constrain. When ADR-014 was written, there was no systematic way to discover that "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements" was a relevant constraint. --- ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that "durable" has at least two materially different meanings in our system** — infrastructure durability (data is persisted) and regulatory durability (data is committed, queryable, and confirmed before client acknowledgement).
