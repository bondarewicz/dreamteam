# Learning Review: FCA Audit Trail Non-Compliance (2026-01-19) ## 1. Situation Snapshot On 2026-01-19, an FCA audit flagged that 6 weeks of order state transition records for regulated instruments were non-compliant with COBS 11.2A audit trail requirements. The root issue was a semantic mismatch between MJ's event-sourced architecture (asynchronous projection to read models) and Bird's domain rule requiring synchronous, immediately-durable commitment to a compliance record store before confirming transitions to clients. Four specific audit queries returned stale state because projection lag had not completed at query time. The gap went undetected for approximately 4 months — from the architecture decision through to the external audit — because no internal process connected domain compliance rules to architecture reviews, and no monitoring existed to detect projection lag from a regulatory perspective. ## 2. Timeline **~2025-09-19 (4 months before incident):** MJ designed order state transitions using event sourcing. ADR-014 was written and reviewed. MJ understood "durable" in the infrastructure sense (fsync'd event log) — a reasonable interpretation for an engineer without regulatory domain context. At this point, MJ had no visibility into Bird's domain rule specifying that durability meant synchronous commitment to a compliance-grade store *before client confirmation*. **~2025-09-19 (same window):** Kobe reviewed ADR-014 for architectural soundness. The review was thorough within its defined scope: scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, security. Kobe's checklist did not include regulatory compliance or domain rule compatibility. Bird was not tagged as a reviewer. This was not an oversight by Kobe — the checklist simply did not have that step, and there was no process requiring it. **2025-09 through 2026-01:** The system operated in production. Projection lag averaged under 200ms, which was functionally invisible for all non-regulatory use cases. No audit trail completeness monitoring existed. From Pippen's operational perspective, the system was healthy — the SLOs being tracked did not include regulatory query freshness. **2026-01-19:** FCA auditors queried order state records within seconds of transitions occurring. In 4 cases, the asynchronous projection had not yet completed, and the audit query returned stale (pre-transition) state. The FCA flagged 6 weeks of records as non-compliant with COBS 11.2A, which requires that transitions be immediately durable and queryable. **Post-incident:** The team discovered the mismatch between the architectural model and the regulatory requirement. Investigation revealed the gap had been structurally invisible — no single person or process was positioned to catch it. ## 3. Contributing Factors Each of the following factors independently contributed to the outcome. Removing any single one might have prevented or detected the issue earlier. They are not a causal chain — they are a web of conditions. **Terminology divergence between domains.** MJ interpreted "immediately durable" as an infrastructure property (data fsync'd to disk), which the event log satisfied. Bird's domain rule meant "synchronously committed to the compliance record store and queryable before the transition is confirmed to the client." These are materially different requirements that use the same word — "durable" — to mean different things. Neither interpretation was wrong in its own context. **ADR review process excluded domain authority.** Kobe's review checklist — the team's standard process for ADR review — covered architectural qualities but did not include a step to identify whether the decision touched regulated domain rules or to tag Bird for domain compliance review. This was a structural gap in the process, not an individual failure. **Bird was not in the review loop.** Bird's domain rule existed and was documented in the domain model under "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements." However, the ADR review process did not require cross-referencing against the domain model, and Bird was not tagged as a reviewer. Bird had no mechanism to know that ADR-014 was being written. **No regulatory query freshness monitoring.** Pippen's operational monitoring covered system health metrics but did not include audit trail completeness or projection lag from a regulatory compliance perspective. The 200ms projection lag was operationally acceptable but regulatorily insufficient. There was no alert that would fire when a regulatory query returned stale state. **The failure mode was silent.** The system did not error. It returned data — just not current-enough data. From the application's perspective, eventual consistency was working as designed. The non-compliance was invisible without either domain-aware monitoring or an external audit. **Deprioritization context.** The absence of regulatory monitoring and domain-tagged ADR reviews was not negligent. These are the kinds of cross-cutting concerns that only become obvious after an incident reveals the gap. The team's existing review and monitoring processes were reasonable for the system's non-regulatory requirements. Regulated-instrument audit trail requirements are a specialized concern that requires explicit, deliberate process support. ## 4.
