# Learning Review: Payment Service Double-Charge During Infrastructure Failover **Incident date**: 2026-03-05 **Review date**: 2026-03-24 --- ## 1. Situation Snapshot During a scheduled infrastructure failover on 2026-03-05, the payment processing service double-charged 312 customers, each billed twice for a single transaction. Total financial exposure was £28,400. The incident spanned a 4-minute failover window but was not detected until a customer complaint 40 minutes later. Refunds were issued within 6 hours of detection. The failure involved the interaction of four independent systems: the payment service, the counter service, the failover runbook, and the monitoring stack. --- ## 2. Timeline | Time (relative) | Event | What was known | Decision / Action | |---|---|---|---| | T+0:00 | Scheduled infrastructure failover begins | Ops team executing planned maintenance per runbook | Services restarted in documented dependency order | | T+0:00 to T+4:00 | Payment service restarts before counter service | Runbook lists counter service as non-critical; ops team has no reason to question this | Payment service comes online, cannot reach counter service, activates fallback: resets attempt_number to 1 | | T+0:00 to T+4:00 | 312 payment requests processed during window | Payment service appears healthy — charges succeed | No intervention; system looks operational | | T+4:00 | Counter service comes back online | Failover complete, all services green | Ops team considers failover successful | | T+44:00 | Customer reports double charge | First signal that something went wrong | Investigation begins | | ~T+50:00 | Engineering confirms 312 duplicate charges | Pattern identified: all duplicates occurred during the failover window with reset idempotency keys | Refund process initiated | | T+6h | All 312 refunds issued | Full scope understood | Incident closed, review scheduled | --- ## 3. Contributing Factors Each of the following conditions was independently necessary for this outcome. Remove any one, and the double-charges do not happen. **Factor 1: The counter service fallback resets attempt_number to 1, producing previously-used idempotency keys.** Shaq implemented this fallback 8 months prior as a defensive measure to keep the payment service operational when the counter service was temporarily unreachable. This was a reasonable resilience decision in the context of brief network blips. The failure mode — that a reset counter could regenerate a key identical to one already used — was not obvious at the time and was not surfaced during review. **Factor 2: The idempotency key scheme encodes attempt_number as its only variable component per order.** The key format (order_id + attempt_number) means that if attempt_number resets, the regenerated key is indistinguishable from the original. A scheme that incorporated a unique token, timestamp, or random component alongside the attempt number would have produced a novel key even after a counter reset. **Factor 3: Stripe's 24-hour idempotency window had already expired for some original charges, or the regenerated keys were novel.** MJ's analysis confirms: the reset counter produced keys that had *not* been previously submitted within Stripe's 24-hour deduplication window. Stripe's safeguard — returning the original charge result for duplicate keys — did not activate because the keys were technically new. The system relied on Stripe as a second line of defense, but the key collision scenario fell outside its protection window. **Factor 4: The failover runbook classified the counter service as non-critical, placing it last in restart order.** The runbook did not document the payment service's dependency on the counter service for idempotency correctness. Kobe had previously updated the runbook for a different dependency three months earlier, but this particular dependency was unknown. The ops team followed the runbook correctly — the runbook itself was incomplete. **Factor 5: The payment service came online and began processing charges before the counter service was available.** The 4-minute gap between payment service startup and counter service startup was the window in which the fallback activated. If the payment service had queued or rejected payment requests until its dependencies were confirmed healthy, no charges would have been processed with reset keys. **Factor 6: No monitoring existed for idempotency key uniqueness or duplicate charge detection.** Pippen's team tracked charge success/failure rates, but nothing in the monitoring stack could detect that two successful charges shared the same underlying order. The double-charge was invisible to every automated system. This is understandable — monitoring what *didn't* fail is harder to prioritize than monitoring what does — but it meant the only detection mechanism was a customer noticing the problem. **Factor 7: Acceptance criteria for the payment service did not cover counter-service-unavailable scenarios.** Bird confirms the domain rule ("a customer must never be charged more than once for a single order") was well understood, but the test suite did not exercise the fallback path. The gap between the domain invariant and its technical enforcement was untested, which meant the violation could ship and persist for 8 months without detection. --- ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that resilience fallbacks can violate domain invariants.** The counter-reset fallback kept the payment service *available* but broke the *correctness* guarantee. This means every fallback path in a payment-critical service must be evaluated not just for "does the service stay up?" but for "does the service still enforce its core domain rules?" - **We now know that idempotency keys must be collision-resistant independent of any single service's state.** A key scheme that can regenerate previously-used values under any reachable system state is not idempotent — it is conditionally idempotent. This means idempotency key generation should incorporate entropy or be derived from immutable, non-resettable sources. - **We now know that runbook dependency ordering is a correctness concern, not just a performance concern.** Restarting a downstream service before its upstream dependency is available can activate fallback paths with unreviewed consequences. This means service dependency graphs must be explicit, auditable, and tested — not maintained ad hoc. - **We now know that monitoring charge success rates is insufficient to detect duplicate charges.** A duplicate charge looks like two successes. This means financial systems need reconciliation-style monitoring that checks business-level invariants (one charge per order), not just technical-level signals (HTTP 200). - **We now know that domain rules and their technical enforcement can drift apart silently over time.** Bird defined the rule; Shaq implemented enforcement; the fallback PR introduced a conditional violation; 8 months passed. This means domain invariants need periodic re-verification against the actual code paths that enforce them, especially after changes to error handling or fallback logic. --- ## 5. Action Items 1. **`[PREVENT]`** — Replace the counter-reset fallback with a fail-closed behavior: if the counter service is unreachable, the payment service must queue or reject payment requests rather than proceeding with a default attempt_number. **Owner: Shaq. Due: 2026-04-04.** 2. **`[PREVENT]`** — Redesign the idempotency key to include a cryptographic nonce or UUID component so that key uniqueness does not depend on counter service availability. **Owner: MJ (design) + Shaq (implementation). Due: 2026-04-11.** 3. **`[PREVENT]`** — Update the failover runbook to classify the counter service as a critical dependency of the payment service, and enforce startup ordering accordingly. **Owner: Kobe. Due: 2026-03-31.** 4. **`[DETECT]`** — Add monitoring that reconciles charges against orders on a rolling basis (e.g., every 5 minutes) and alerts when any order has more than one successful charge. **Owner: Pippen. Due: 2026-04-11.** 5. **`[DETECT]`** — Add an alert on idempotency key fallback activation — any time the counter-reset path fires, it should page immediately, since it indicates the payment service is operating in a degraded correctness mode. **Owner: Pippen. Due: 2026-04-04.** 6. **`[MITIGATE]`** — Implement an automatic duplicate-charge refund mechanism: if reconciliation detects a double charge, initiate a refund without waiting for a customer complaint. **Owner: Shaq. Due: 2026-04-18.** 7. **`[PROCESS]`** — Add "counter-service-unavailable" as a mandatory test scenario in the payment service acceptance criteria. More broadly, require that every domain invariant identified by Bird has at least one test exercising its enforcement under degraded-dependency conditions. **Owner: Bird (criteria) + Shaq (tests). Due: 2026-04-11.** 8. **`[PROCESS]`** — Establish a quarterly review where domain invariants are cross-referenced against their technical enforcement paths, specifically checking whether any fallback or error-handling code can violate them. **Owner: Bird + MJ. First review: 2026-04-30.** --- ## 6.
