Let me analyze these requirements. There's a classic CAP theorem tension here between requirements 2 (strong consistency) and 3 (high availability). Let me think through this carefully. Requirements: 1. 200ms latency for courier assignment 2. No double-assignment (strong consistency) 3. Available during DB node failure (high availability) 4. Small team (2 engineers) The CAP theorem tension between #2 and #3 is real but not necessarily a full contradiction — it depends on the partition tolerance expectations. In practice, systems like this can achieve strong consistency AND high availability for most failure modes using synchronous replication with automatic failover (e.g., PostgreSQL with synchronous standby). The 200ms latency constraint is definitely achievable. During a network partition, I can't truly have both strong consistency and availability, but a single node failure with synchronous replication and automatic failover can maintain both consistency and availability through the failover window. I should frame this as a manageable trade-off rather than a blocker — the architecture can handle most failure scenarios, but the team needs to understand that during actual network partitions, they'd need to prioritize consistency over availability to prevent double-assignment. Let me design an architecture around PostgreSQL with synchronous replication and automatic failover to handle this. The key distinction is that node failure with synchronous replication and automatic failover is actually achievable without sacrificing consistency, so this isn't a true CAP contradiction—just a careful architectural choice. I'll proceed with the design and call out the trade-off explicitly.
