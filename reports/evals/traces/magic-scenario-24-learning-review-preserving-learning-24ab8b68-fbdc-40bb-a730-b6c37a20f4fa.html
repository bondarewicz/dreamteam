# Learning Review: FCA Audit Trail Non-Compliance (2026-01-19) ## 1. Situation Snapshot On 2026-01-19, an FCA audit flagged that six weeks of order state transition records for regulated instruments were non-compliant with COBS 11.2A audit trail requirements. The root issue was a semantic mismatch: MJ's event-sourced architecture satisfied database-level durability (fsync) but not regulatory-level durability (synchronous commitment to a compliance store before client confirmation). Four specific audit queries returned stale state because asynchronous projections had not yet completed. The compliance gap affected all order state transitions recorded since the architecture went live, exposing the firm to regulatory sanction for the entire period. ## 2. Timeline **~2025-09-19 (approximately 4 months before incident):** MJ designs order state transitions using event sourcing. ADR-014 is written, documenting the pattern: events written to an append-only log, projected asynchronously into read models. MJ understands "durable" in the infrastructure sense — data fsync'd to disk, recoverable after crash. This understanding is reasonable from a pure systems architecture perspective and is not questioned during review. **~2025-09-19 (same review cycle):** Kobe reviews ADR-014. The review is thorough on its own terms — scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, security are all evaluated. Event sourcing passes on all four dimensions. Kobe's checklist does not include a step to cross-reference domain rules or tag Bird for regulatory compliance review. Bird is not tagged as a reviewer and does not see the ADR. At this moment, no one involved in the decision is aware that a pre-existing domain rule imposes a stricter definition of "durable." **~2025-09-19 onward (6 weeks of exposure):** The system operates normally. Projection lag hovers around 200ms — imperceptible to users and well within operational tolerances. No monitoring exists to detect whether audit-queryable state lags behind committed events, because the team does not yet understand this as a distinct failure mode. From Pippen's operational perspective, the system is healthy. **2026-01-19 (incident day):** FCA conducts an audit. Regulators query state records within seconds of transitions occurring. In 4 cases, the asynchronous projection has not completed, and the audit query returns stale (pre-transition) state. The FCA flags this as non-compliant with COBS 11.2A, which requires state transitions to be "immediately durable and queryable." The firm learns of the gap from the regulator, not from internal monitoring. ## 3. Contributing Factors Each of the following factors was independently necessary for this outcome. Removing any single one would likely have prevented or detected the issue before regulatory exposure. **Terminology divergence between architecture and regulation.** MJ interpreted "immediately durable" through a database engineering lens (fsync, crash recovery). Bird's domain rule meant "synchronously committed to the compliance record store before confirming the transition to the client." Both interpretations are internally consistent within their respective domains. The word "durable" carried two different technical meanings, and no process existed to detect or reconcile this divergence. **Absent cross-domain review step.** Kobe's ADR review checklist is well-scoped for architectural quality but does not include regulatory compliance or domain rule compatibility. This is not a gap that was deprioritized — it was a gap that was never identified. The checklist was designed for engineering concerns, and regulatory cross-referencing was not part of its mental model. **Bird was not tagged for ADR-014 review.** Bird's domain rules — including the COBS 11.2A audit requirement — were documented in the domain model under "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements." However, no mechanism existed to surface these rules when adjacent architectural decisions were made. Bird's absence from the review was not an oversight by any individual; it was a structural gap. No one knew to tag Bird because the connection between "event sourcing projection model" and "regulatory audit trail timing" was not obvious until after the incident. **No audit trail completeness monitoring.** Pippen's operational monitoring covered the system's health from a service perspective — uptime, latency, error rates. It did not include a specific check for "can a regulator query the current state of a transition within the required window?" This monitoring gap existed because the team did not model "regulatory queryability" as a distinct operational concern separate from "read model freshness." **Projection lag was invisible under normal load.** At 200ms typical lag, the asynchronous model appeared to work perfectly for all user-facing and internal purposes. The failure mode — a regulator querying within milliseconds of a transition — was outside the scenarios anyone had reason to test or monitor. The system was reliable in all contexts except the one that mattered for compliance. ## 4. Learnings **We now know that "durable" means different things in infrastructure and regulatory contexts,** which means any architecture decision touching data persistence for regulated instruments must be explicitly validated against the regulatory definition — not just the database definition — before approval. **We now know that ADR reviews that are limited to engineering concerns can silently approve architectures that violate domain rules,** which means the review process must include a mandatory cross-reference against the domain model for any decision that changes how regulated data is stored, queried, or timed. **We now know that Bird's domain rules are load-bearing constraints that must be actively surfaced during architectural decisions, not passively available in documentation,** which means Bird (or a designated domain authority) must be a required reviewer on any ADR that touches regulated data flows — the burden is on the process to pull domain rules in, not on Bird to monitor all ADRs. **We now know that projection lag is a regulatory risk, not just a performance metric,** which means for regulated instruments, read model freshness must be monitored against regulatory SLAs (e.g., "queryable within N seconds of commitment"), with alerting that fires before a regulator finds the gap. **We now know that our monitoring did not distinguish between "system healthy" and "system compliant,"** which means compliance observability is a distinct concern that must be designed, instrumented, and owned separately from operational health. ## 5.
