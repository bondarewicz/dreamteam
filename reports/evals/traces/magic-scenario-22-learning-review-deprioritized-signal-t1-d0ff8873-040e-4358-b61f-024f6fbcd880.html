# Learning Review: Invoicing Service — Malformed supplier_id Reconciliation Failure **Date of incident**: 2026-02-28 **Review date**: 2026-03-26 --- ## 1. Situation Snapshot The Invoicing Service ingests supplier invoice data via an external webhook. Starting 2026-02-28, a subset of invoice records were persisted with float-formatted `supplier_id` values (e.g., `"12345.0"` instead of `"12345"`), caused by a new sub-provider onboarded by the webhook partner. The records were syntactically valid and wrote successfully, but the downstream reconciliation job — which performs exact-string matching on `supplier_id` — silently failed to match these records for 3 days. The discrepancy was not detected by automated monitoring; it was caught by the finance team during month-end close, resulting in a £47,000 discrepancy in the monthly reconciliation report. No data was lost, but manual remediation was required. --- ## 2. Contributing Factors Each of these factors independently contributed to the outcome. Removing any single one would likely have prevented or significantly reduced the impact. 1. **No input normalisation on `supplier_id` at the ingestion boundary.** The webhook accepted and persisted free-text strings without canonicalisation. Bird's recommended normalisation step (TD-291) would have made the system resilient to format variations regardless of upstream behaviour. 2. **Upstream change without notification.** The webhook provider onboarded a new sub-provider whose system generates float-formatted IDs. This change was not communicated to downstream consumers. The team's risk acceptance was predicated on the provider's assurance that integer-formatted IDs were the production norm — an assurance that became stale without anyone knowing. 3. **Exact-string matching in the reconciliation job with no fuzzy or canonical comparison.** The reconciliation logic treated `"12345"` and `"12345.0"` as entirely different identifiers. A canonical comparison (or a shared normalisation function used by both writer and reader) would have matched them. 4. **Silent failure mode in the reconciliation job.** The job logged WARNINGs for unmatched IDs but did not alert. The warning volume was low enough to fall below the manual review threshold. The system's observability was designed around volume thresholds, not semantic significance — a single unmatched financial record is significant regardless of volume. 5. **TD-291 remained in the backlog across a sprint boundary.** The deprioritisation was reasonable at the time, but no mechanism existed to reassess the risk when the webhook integration went live and began handling real traffic. The tech debt item was treated as static rather than as a risk that compounds once the system is in production. --- ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that upstream provider assurances about data format are point-in-time statements, not contracts.** This means any system that accepts external input must defend its own invariants at the boundary, regardless of what the provider says they will send. Provider behaviour can change without notice when they onboard sub-providers, update systems, or change configuration. - **We now know that WARNING-level logs for financial data mismatches are insufficient as a detection mechanism.** A single unmatched invoice record has monetary significance. This means financial reconciliation anomalies need to alert on *occurrence*, not on *volume threshold*. - **We now know that deprioritised data quality risks compound once a system goes live.** TD-291 was a reasonable deprioritisation during development. Once the webhook was processing real invoices, the risk profile changed — but no re-evaluation was triggered. This means tech debt items that represent data integrity risks need a reassessment gate at go-live. - **We now know that normalisation must be a shared concern between the writer and the reader.** When one service writes a value and another service matches on it, they must agree on canonical form. This means normalisation should happen at ingestion (so the canonical form is what gets persisted), not at query time. --- ## 5. Backfill existing malformed records. — **Owner: Shaq / Engineering. This generalises beyond `supplier_id` to any external identifier. — **Owner: Shaq / Engineering. Target: next sprint.** 3. `[DETECT]` **Change reconciliation job unmatched-ID warnings to alerts.** Any non-zero count of unmatched financial records should trigger an alert to the on-call engineer and notify the finance team within 1 business day. — **Owner: Pippen / Operations. Target: 1 week.** 4. `[DETECT]` **Add a data quality monitor** that compares ingested `supplier_id` values against known canonical patterns and alerts on format drift (e.g., first occurrence of a float-formatted ID). — **Owner: Engineering. Target: 2 sprints.** 5. `[MITIGATE]` **Reconciliation job should attempt canonical matching as a fallback** when exact matching fails — e.g., trim trailing zeros, compare numeric equivalence — and flag the match as "fuzzy" for human review. — **Owner: Engineering. Target: next sprint.** 6. `[PROCESS]` **Introduce a go-live risk reassessment gate.** When a system moves from development to production, all deprioritised risk items (especially data quality and integration risks) must be reviewed.
