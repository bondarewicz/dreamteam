# Learning Review: FCA Audit Trail Non-Compliance (2026-01-19) ## 1. Situation Snapshot During a routine FCA audit on 2026-01-19, regulators discovered that 6 weeks of order state transition records for regulated instruments did not meet COBS 11.2A audit trail requirements. The core issue: order state transitions were recorded via an asynchronous event-sourcing projection pipeline (typically ~200ms lag), while the regulatory requirement demanded synchronous, immediately-durable writes to a compliance record store *before* confirming the transition to the client. Four specific audit queries returned stale state because projection had not yet completed. The gap affected all regulated instrument order transitions over the 6-week window, creating a compliance exposure with the FCA. ## 2. Timeline **~2025-09-19 (4 months before incident):** MJ designed the order state transition architecture using event sourcing. State changes flow to an event log (fsync'd, durable in the database sense) and project asynchronously into a read model. MJ authored ADR-014 documenting this decision. At this point, MJ understood "durable" to mean disk-level persistence — which the event log satisfies — not "synchronously committed to a regulatory-grade queryable store before client confirmation." **~2025-09-19 to 2025-10-XX (ADR-014 review period):** Kobe reviewed ADR-014 for architectural soundness: scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, security. The review concluded the pattern was appropriate. Bird was not tagged as a reviewer. Kobe's review checklist did not include a step to verify domain-rule compliance or to involve Bird. This made sense at the time — the checklist was designed for technical architecture review, and regulatory compliance had not previously been a source of architectural constraint in this area. **2025-10-XX to 2026-01-19 (6 weeks of non-compliant operation):** The system operated with asynchronous projections. Projection lag of ~200ms was not a practical problem for normal product operation. No audit trail completeness monitoring existed, so the gap between "event logged" and "projection queryable" was invisible to the team. There was no signal that anything was wrong. **2026-01-19 (incident):** FCA auditors queried order state transition records. In 4 cases, queries arrived within seconds of a transition, before projection had completed. The returned state was stale. The FCA flagged the entire 6-week window as non-compliant with COBS 11.2A, because the architecture *by design* could not guarantee immediate queryability — the 4 observed failures were symptoms of a structural gap, not edge cases. ## 3. Contributing Factors Each of these factors independently contributed to the outcome. No single one is "the root cause." **Terminology divergence between architecture and domain.** MJ interpreted "immediately durable" through a database-engineering lens (fsync to disk). Bird's domain rule meant "synchronously committed to the compliance record store and queryable before confirming to the client." The same English phrase carried two precise but different technical meanings. Neither interpretation was wrong in its own context. **Bird was not included in ADR-014 review.** The domain rule predated ADR-014 and was documented in the domain model under "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements." But there was no mechanism to surface domain-model constraints during architecture review. Bird was not tagged because the review process did not call for it — not because anyone judged Bird's input unnecessary. **Kobe's review checklist scoped to technical architecture.** The checklist covered scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, and security. Regulatory compliance and domain-rule compatibility were outside scope. This scoping was reasonable for a technical review — but it left a gap where architecture decisions affecting regulated domains had no compliance checkpoint. **No audit trail completeness monitoring.** Pippen's operational setup did not include alerting on projection lag relative to regulatory query windows. The 200ms lag was well within product SLAs, so it was not flagged. The absence of compliance-specific monitoring meant the gap could only be discovered externally — by the FCA itself. **Domain rules and architecture decisions lived in separate artifacts with no cross-referencing.** Bird's regulatory rules were in the domain model. MJ's architecture decisions were in ADRs. There was no linking mechanism, dependency tag, or automated check to ensure new ADRs were evaluated against applicable domain constraints. ## 4. Learnings **We now know that "durable" means different things across architecture and regulatory-domain contexts,** which means any architecture decision involving state persistence for regulated entities must include an explicit definition of durability that both the architect and the domain authority sign off on. **We now know that domain rules can impose hard architectural constraints that are invisible to a purely technical review,** which means architecture reviews for regulated domains must include a domain-compliance checkpoint — technical soundness alone is insufficient. **We now know that the absence of compliance-specific monitoring creates silent risk,** which means regulatory query-path guarantees (latency, completeness, consistency) must be monitored and alerted on independently from product SLAs. **We now know that Bird's non-participation in ADR-014 was a process gap, not a judgment call,** which means the review process itself must encode which roles are mandatory reviewers based on the domain affected, rather than relying on the author to know who to tag. **We now know that 200ms of acceptable product latency can be 200ms of regulatory non-compliance,** which means product performance metrics and regulatory compliance metrics are separate concerns that must be tracked and alerted on separately. ## 5.
