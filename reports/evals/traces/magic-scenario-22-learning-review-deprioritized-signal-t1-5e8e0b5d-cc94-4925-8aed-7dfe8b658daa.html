# Learning Review: Invoicing Service Supplier ID Mismatch (2026-02-28) ## Situation Snapshot The Invoicing Service, which ingests supplier data via an external webhook, began persisting invoice records with malformed `supplier_id` values on 2026-02-28. Some supplier IDs arrived as float-formatted strings (e.g., `"12345.0"`) instead of the expected integer-formatted strings (`"12345"`). While ingestion and persistence succeeded without error, the downstream reconciliation job — which performs exact-string matching on `supplier_id` — silently failed to match these records for 3 days. The discrepancy was discovered by the finance team during month-end close, not by automated monitoring, resulting in a £47,000 gap in the monthly reconciliation report. ## Timeline 1. **~6 weeks before incident (mid-January 2026)**: During spec review, Bird identified that the external webhook does not guarantee format consistency for `supplier_id` and recommended a normalisation step before persistence. This was flagged as a medium-priority data quality risk. 2. **Same period**: Coach K logged the recommendation as tech debt item TD-291 and deprioritised it. The webhook integration was already behind schedule, and adding normalisation would have pushed go-live by 3 days. The webhook provider had confirmed their production system sends integer-formatted IDs. At the time, accepting this risk was a reasonable schedule trade-off against a scenario that the provider's own confirmation suggested was unlikely. 3. **Sprint delivery**: Shaq implemented webhook ingestion per the spec. `supplier_id` was accepted as a string and persisted directly. TD-291 was explicitly out-of-scope; Shaq was aware of it and correctly did not implement normalisation. The implementation matched the agreed scope. 4. **Between delivery and incident**: The webhook provider onboarded a new sub-provider whose system generates float-formatted IDs. This change was not communicated to the team. The original risk assessment — based on the provider's confirmation of integer formatting — was silently invalidated by a change in the provider's own supply chain. 5. **2026-02-28**: Float-formatted `supplier_id` values began arriving via the webhook and were persisted successfully. No ingestion errors surfaced. 6. **2026-02-28 through 2026-03-02 (3 days)**: The reconciliation job ran daily. It logged `WARNING`-level messages for unmatched `supplier_id` values, but the volume was below the manual review threshold and no alert fired. The mismatch accumulated silently. 7. **~2026-03-03 (month-end close)**: The finance team identified a £47,000 discrepancy in the monthly reconciliation report. Investigation traced it back to the `supplier_id` format mismatch. ## Contributing Factors Each of these factors independently contributed to the outcome. Removing any single one would likely have prevented or significantly reduced the impact. 1. **No input normalisation on `supplier_id`**: The webhook accepted and persisted raw string values without canonicalisation. The domain model treated `supplier_id` as an opaque string, which is correct in principle, but "opaque" was interpreted as "no transformation needed" rather than "normalise to a canonical opaque form." The risk of format inconsistency was known but unmitigated. 2. **Accepted risk materialised through an unanticipated vector**: The deprioritisation of TD-291 was based on the webhook provider's confirmation that their production system sends integer IDs. This was accurate at the time. The risk materialised not because the provider changed their behaviour, but because they onboarded a new sub-provider — a scenario outside the boundary of the original risk assessment. 3. **No upstream change notification**: The webhook provider did not announce the onboarding of a new sub-provider with different ID formatting. The team had no mechanism to detect or be informed of changes in the provider's supply chain that would affect data format. 4. **Silent failure in the reconciliation job**: The reconciliation job logged warnings for unmatched `supplier_id` values but did not alert. The job's contract was "match or warn," not "match or escalate." This meant the failure mode was accumulation of quiet warnings rather than a visible error. 5. **Warning volume below review threshold**: The volume of unmatched-ID warnings was low enough that it did not trigger manual review. The threshold was calibrated for noise suppression, not for detecting a new class of systematic mismatch. A small number of mismatches looked like normal noise, not a signal. 6. **Detection depended on a monthly human process**: The £47,000 discrepancy was caught by the finance team during month-end close — the only point in the pipeline where the accumulated error became visible. There was no automated check between daily reconciliation and monthly close that would have surfaced a growing delta. ## Learnings - **We now know that "opaque string" in the domain model does not mean "no transformation needed."** It means the system should not interpret the value, but it should still normalise it to a canonical form before persistence. Any field used as a join key downstream must have a single canonical representation, even if the domain treats it as opaque. - **We now know that risk assessments based on a third party's current behaviour are brittle.** The provider's confirmation was accurate, but their supply chain changed without notice. When we accept risk based on an external party's stated behaviour, we are also implicitly accepting the risk that their behaviour changes without notification. This should be factored into the severity assessment. - **We now know that a WARNING log without an alert is functionally equivalent to no detection.** If a failure mode is important enough to log, it is important enough to alert on — or at minimum, to aggregate and surface via a threshold. Warnings that are only discovered during manual log review are post-hoc forensics, not monitoring. - **We now know that silent reconciliation failures can accumulate to material financial impact within days.** A 3-day detection gap produced a £47,000 discrepancy. The blast radius of a silent mismatch is proportional to the time between occurrence and detection, multiplied by transaction volume. Any reconciliation pipeline needs a "freshness and completeness" check, not just a "did the job run" check. - **We now know that deprioritising a known data-quality risk is qualitatively different from deprioritising a feature.** A missing feature is visible; a missing validation is invisible until it fails. Tech debt items that address silent failure modes should be weighted differently in prioritisation than items that address missing functionality. ## Action Items 1. `[PREVENT]` **Implement `supplier_id` normalisation on webhook ingestion** — strip trailing `.0`, enforce integer-string canonical form before persistence. Owner: Shaq. Deadline: 2026-03-28. This closes TD-291. 2. `[PREVENT]` **Backfill existing malformed `supplier_id` records** — run a one-time migration to canonicalise all `supplier_id` values currently stored with float formatting. Owner: Shaq. Deadline: 2026-03-28. 3. `[DETECT]` **Add alerting on reconciliation mismatch rate** — the reconciliation job should emit a metric for unmatched records per run. Alert when the count exceeds zero (or a very low threshold) for two consecutive runs. Owner: Pippen. Deadline: 2026-04-04. 4. `[DETECT]` **Add a daily reconciliation completeness check** — an automated job that compares the count of ingested invoices against the count of successfully reconciled invoices and alerts on any delta exceeding a configurable threshold. Owner: Pippen. Deadline: 2026-04-04. 5. `[MITIGATE]` **Add a webhook input validation layer** — for all fields used as downstream join keys, validate format on ingestion and reject or quarantine records that do not match the expected canonical form, rather than persisting silently. Owner: Shaq. Deadline: 2026-04-11. 6. `[PROCESS]` **Introduce a "silent failure risk" tag for tech debt items** — tech debt items that address silent failure modes (data quality, reconciliation, monitoring gaps) should be tagged distinctly from feature-gap tech debt. During sprint planning, silent-failure items should be evaluated on a shorter acceptable-deferral horizon. Owner: Coach K. Deadline: 2026-04-04. 7. `[PROCESS]` **Request change-notification SLA from webhook provider** — negotiate a contractual or operational commitment from the provider to notify us of changes to their data format, sub-provider onboarding, or any change in the upstream system that affects the structure of data sent via the webhook. Owner: Coach K. Deadline: 2026-04-11. ## Preserving the Learning - **ADR: Canonical Form for External Identifiers** — Document the decision that all externally-sourced identifier fields used as join keys must be normalised to a canonical string form on ingestion, regardless of whether the domain model treats them as opaque. Include the `supplier_id` incident as the motivating example. Owner: Bird. Deadline: 2026-04-04. - **Domain Rule Addition (Bird's domain spec)**: "Any field that crosses a system boundary and is used for downstream matching MUST pass through a normalisation step. The domain model defines the canonical form; the ingestion layer enforces it." Owner: Bird. Deadline: 2026-03-31. - **Runbook Update: Reconciliation Job** — Add a section on interpreting `supplier_id` mismatch warnings, the expected alert behaviour, and the escalation path. Include the pattern of "low-volume warnings masking systematic mismatch." Owner: Pippen. Deadline: 2026-04-04. - **Sprint Planning Checklist Item**: "Are there any deprioritised tech debt items tagged `silent-failure-risk` older than one sprint? If yes, review and explicitly re-accept or schedule." Owner: Coach K. Ongoing.
