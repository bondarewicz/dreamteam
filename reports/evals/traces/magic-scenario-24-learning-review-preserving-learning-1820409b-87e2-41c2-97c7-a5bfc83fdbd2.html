# Learning Review: FCA Audit Trail Non-Compliance (2026-01-19) ## 1. Situation Snapshot On 2026-01-19, the FCA flagged that 6 weeks of order state transition records for regulated instruments were non-compliant with COBS 11.2A audit trail requirements. The gap originated from a mismatch between MJ's event-sourced architecture (asynchronous projection to read models) and Bird's domain rule requiring synchronous, immediately-durable commitment to a compliance record store before client confirmation. The issue affected audit queryability — in at least 4 observed cases, regulators received stale state because the projection had not yet completed. The compliance exposure window spanned roughly 6 weeks of production operation. No financial penalty has been reported at time of writing, but the regulatory relationship and audit posture were materially impacted. ## 2. Timeline **~2025-09-19 (4 months before incident):** MJ designs order state transitions using event sourcing. ADR-014 is written and reviewed. MJ understands "durable" in the infrastructure sense — events are fsync'd to the event log. The asynchronous projection to the read model introduces a small lag (typically under 200ms). At this point, MJ is not aware that Bird's domain model imposes a stricter, regulatory definition of "immediately durable" that requires synchronous commitment to a compliance-grade store before confirming to the client. **~2025-09-19 (same window):** Kobe reviews ADR-014. The review focuses on architectural soundness — scalability, failure modes, operational complexity, security. The review checklist does not include a step to check regulatory compliance or tag Bird for domain rule compatibility review. Bird is not tagged and does not participate. This makes sense at the time: Kobe's checklist was designed for technical architecture review, not regulatory cross-referencing. There is no process that connects ADR review to domain model compliance obligations. **~2025-09-19 onward:** The system goes to production. The 200ms projection lag is not operationally problematic. No audit trail completeness monitoring is deployed, so the gap between event log writes and read model projection is invisible to operations. **~2026-01-05 to 2026-01-19 (approximate audit window):** FCA auditors query order state transition records. In at least 4 cases, queries arrive within seconds of a state transition and the projection has not yet completed. The audit query returns stale state. The compliance record store (the projected read model) does not yet reflect the transition. The auditors flag this as non-compliant with COBS 11.2A. **2026-01-19:** The FCA formally flags non-compliance. The team discovers the issue through the regulator, not through internal monitoring. ## 3. Contributing Factors Each of the following factors independently contributed to the outcome. No single one is "the root cause" — remove any one and the incident likely still occurs in a different form. It took all of them converging for this to happen silently for 6 weeks. 1. **Terminology divergence between architecture and domain.** MJ and Bird used the word "durable" to mean fundamentally different things. MJ meant infrastructure durability (fsync to disk). Bird meant regulatory durability (synchronous commitment to a compliance-queryable store before client confirmation). Neither definition is wrong — they serve different audiences. The problem is that no process existed to detect and reconcile this kind of semantic gap before it became load-bearing. 2. **ADR review process excluded domain compliance review.** Kobe's ADR review checklist was designed for architectural quality, not regulatory compatibility. This is a reasonable scope for a technical review — adding every possible concern to a single checklist would make it unusable. But the result was that no one with domain authority over regulated instruments ever evaluated whether asynchronous projection was compatible with COBS 11.2A. The process had no handoff point to Bird. 3. **Bird was not tagged as a reviewer on ADR-014.** Bird's domain rule predated the ADR. The rule was documented in the domain model under "Regulated Instruments — Audit Requirements." But the ADR review process had no mechanism to identify which domain rules might be affected by an architecture decision, and therefore no mechanism to pull in the right domain reviewer. Bird's absence from the review was a process gap, not an oversight by any individual. 4. **No audit trail completeness monitoring.** Pippen's operational monitoring did not include a check for projection lag relative to regulatory query windows. The 200ms lag was well within normal operational tolerances, so it was never flagged. Without monitoring that specifically validates "can a regulator query this state transition right now and get the correct answer?", the gap was invisible until the FCA found it. 5. **The async projection model was technically sound but contextually inappropriate.** Event sourcing with async projection is a well-established, appropriate pattern for many use cases. MJ's design was architecturally clean. The mismatch is not that the pattern was bad — it is that it was applied in a context where a domain constraint required synchronous write-through to a compliance store, and that context was never surfaced during design. ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that "durable" has at least two meanings in our system — infrastructure durability and regulatory durability — which means any architecture decision involving state persistence for regulated instruments must explicitly address both definitions.** Assuming a shared understanding of key terms across architecture and domain is unsafe when regulatory obligations are in play. - **We now know that ADR review and domain rule review are separate concerns that must be explicitly connected, which means the ADR process needs a gate that asks "which domain rules does this decision interact with?" and routes to the appropriate domain authority.** A technically excellent architecture review is necessary but not sufficient when the system operates under regulatory constraints. - **We now know that projection lag is not just a performance metric but a compliance metric, which means operational monitoring for regulated data flows must include regulatory queryability SLAs, not just throughput and latency SLAs.** - **We now know that the absence of a domain reviewer on an ADR is itself a risk signal, which means the review process must make it visible when no domain authority has signed off — rather than allowing silent omission.** - **We now know that we discovered this gap through the regulator rather than through our own systems, which means our detection posture for compliance drift is insufficient. The most dangerous compliance failures are the ones where the system appears to work correctly until an external audit reveals otherwise.** ## 5. Action Items 1. **`[PREVENT]` Add a "Regulatory Impact" section to the ADR template.** Every ADR must include a section that explicitly asks: "Does this decision affect state transitions, data retention, or queryability for regulated instruments? If yes, which domain rules apply?" If the author marks "yes" or "unsure," Bird is automatically tagged as a required reviewer. **Owner: MJ. Due: 2026-02-07.** 2. **`[PREVENT]` Add "Domain Rule Compatibility" to Kobe's ADR review checklist.** A new checklist item: "Have all affected domain rules been identified? Has the relevant domain authority (Bird) reviewed for compatibility?" This does not require Kobe to evaluate regulatory compliance directly — only to verify that the right reviewer has been engaged. **Owner: Kobe. Due: 2026-02-07.** 3. **`[PREVENT]` Implement synchronous write-through to the compliance record store for regulated instrument state transitions.** For COBS 11.2A-covered transitions, the state must be committed to a compliance-queryable store synchronously, before confirmation to the client. The event log can continue to serve as the source of truth for replay and projection, but the compliance store must be written in the same transaction boundary. **Owner: MJ (architecture), Shaq (implementation). Due: 2026-02-21.** 4. **`[DETECT]` Deploy audit trail completeness monitoring.** A continuous check that, for every state transition on a regulated instrument, the compliance record store reflects the transition within a configurable SLA (e.g., 0ms for synchronous write-through, with alerting on any lag). This monitor must alert Pippen's operational dashboard and page on-call if the SLA is breached. **Owner: Pippen. Due: 2026-02-14.** 5. **`[DETECT]` Implement a synthetic audit query test.** A scheduled job that performs a state transition on a test regulated instrument and immediately queries the compliance store to verify the transition is present. Due: 2026-02-14.** 6. **`[MITIGATE]` Backfill the 6-week compliance gap.** Replay the event log for the affected 6-week window and generate a complete, auditor-queryable record of all state transitions. Deliver to compliance team for FCA remediation. **Owner: MJ (replay design), Shaq (execution). Due: 2026-01-31.** 7. **`[PROCESS]` Establish a quarterly domain-rule-to-architecture alignment review.** Bird and MJ meet quarterly to walk through recent ADRs and upcoming domain rule changes, specifically looking for mismatches between architectural assumptions and regulatory requirements. **Owner: Magic (facilitation), Bird and MJ (participation). Due: first session by 2026-02-28.** ## 6.
