Key tensions: - 200ms latency for assignment - Strong consistency (no double-assignment) - High availability despite DB node failure - Small team (2 engineers) CAP theorem: Requirements 2 and 3 are in tension. Strong consistency + high availability during partition/failure is the classic CAP impossibility. This is a real contradictory requirement situation. Actually, let me think more carefully. "Available even if a database node fails" doesn't necessarily mean partition tolerance in the CAP sense. It could mean they want redundancy through replicas with synchronous replication and automatic failover—that gives you strong consistency and availability for single-node failures without needing to tolerate network partitions. The real challenge is whether synchronous replication can meet the 200ms latency requirement, and whether failover can happen fast enough to maintain availability. I think this is actually solvable with the right architecture: an in-memory cache layer for fast assignment lookups, distributed locking to prevent double-assignment, and a replicated database with automatic failover for durability and availability. But there's a deeper tension here—strong consistency and high availability during failures are fundamentally at odds per CAP theorem. If we're talking about a network partition, you have to choose between rejecting requests (consistent but unavailable) or risking double-assignment (available but inconsistent). However, if the requirement is specifically about database node failures rather than network partitions, synchronous replication with failover can actually maintain both. The real question is whether "stay available" means zero downtime or just quick recovery—that distinction changes everything about whether this is truly contradictory or just tight.
