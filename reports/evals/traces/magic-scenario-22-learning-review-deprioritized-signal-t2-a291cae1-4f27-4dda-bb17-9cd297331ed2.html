# Learning Review: Invoicing Service Supplier ID Mismatch (2026-02-28) ## 1. Situation Snapshot On 2026-02-28, the Invoicing Service began persisting invoice records with malformed `supplier_id` values after an external webhook started sending float-formatted IDs (e.g., `"12345.0"` instead of `"12345"`). The malformed records were valid at the persistence layer but caused silent failures in a downstream reconciliation job that relies on exact-string matching. The mismatch went undetected for 3 days, producing a £47,000 discrepancy in the monthly reconciliation report. The issue was discovered by the finance team during month-end close — not by engineering monitoring. ## 2. Timeline | When | What was known | Decision made | Why it made sense | |------|---------------|---------------|-------------------| | ~2026-01-16 (spec review) | Bird identified that the external webhook does not guarantee format consistency for `supplier_id`. Flagged normalisation as a medium-priority data quality risk. | Recommendation logged as TD-291, normalisation step proposed before persistence. | Bird's domain analysis correctly identified the contract gap — the webhook schema treated `supplier_id` as free-text with no format constraint. | | ~2026-01-16 (sprint planning) | Webhook integration was behind schedule. Webhook provider had confirmed their production system sends integer-formatted IDs. | Coach K deprioritised TD-291 to avoid a 3-day slip on go-live. Risk accepted with plan to address next sprint. | The go-live deadline was a real constraint. The provider's confirmation reduced the perceived likelihood. Accepting a medium-priority risk against a concrete schedule pressure is a normal and defensible trade-off. | | ~2026-01-16 to 2026-02-28 (implementation & operation) | Shaq implemented webhook ingestion per spec, without normalisation, as directed. TD-291 sat in the backlog. | No normalisation added. `supplier_id` persisted as-is from webhook payload. | Shaq followed the agreed scope. Adding out-of-scope work unilaterally would have been a different kind of process failure. | | ~Late February (unknown exact date) | Webhook provider onboarded a new sub-provider whose system generates float-formatted IDs. Contributing Factors Each of the following factors was independently necessary for the incident to occur. Removing any one of them would have prevented or significantly reduced the impact. 1. **No input normalisation at the ingestion boundary.** The webhook handler accepted and persisted `supplier_id` as-is, without canonicalising the format. The known risk (TD-291) had been identified but not yet implemented. 2. **Upstream provider made an unannounced change.** The webhook provider onboarded a new sub-provider whose system emits float-formatted IDs. This was not communicated to downstream consumers. The team's risk assessment was based on a provider confirmation that turned out to apply only to their original system, not to their sub-provider network. 3. **The reconciliation job failed silently.** Exact-string matching on `supplier_id` is inherently brittle — `"12345"` and `"12345.0"` refer to the same entity but do not match. The job logged warnings but did not alert, and the warning volume was below the threshold that would trigger manual review. 4. **TD-291 was not revisited after the initial deprioritisation.** The plan was to address it "next sprint," but it remained in the backlog. There was no mechanism to escalate aging tech debt items that carry data-quality risk, and no trigger to re-evaluate the risk when the webhook went live. 5. **No contract enforcement or schema validation on the webhook input.** The external webhook's `supplier_id` field was free-text with no format constraint. The system trusted the upstream format without defensive validation — the domain's requirement for a canonical identifier format was not enforced at the boundary. ## 4. Learnings - **We now know that upstream provider confirmations about data format are point-in-time statements, not durable guarantees.** This means defensive normalisation at ingestion boundaries should not be gated on whether the current provider "usually" sends the right format — it should be treated as a required invariant enforcement, because upstream systems change without notice. - **We now know that exact-string matching on identifiers sourced from external systems is brittle by default.** This means any reconciliation or join logic that depends on string equality of externally-sourced IDs needs either upstream normalisation or match-time canonicalisation — ideally both. - **We now know that WARNING-level logs without alerting create a false sense of observability.** The reconciliation job appeared to have monitoring (it logged warnings), but in practice no human or automated system acted on those warnings. A warning that nobody sees is functionally equivalent to no warning. - **We now know that deprioritised data-quality risks need an explicit re-evaluation trigger.** TD-291 was deprioritised with a reasonable rationale, but the rationale had an implicit assumption (provider sends integers) that could become invalid at any time. Without a mechanism to revisit, the risk acceptance became permanent by default. ## 5. Action Items - **Implement `supplier_id` normalisation at the webhook ingestion boundary** — strip trailing `.0`, enforce canonical integer-string format before persistence. This is TD-291. `[PREVENT]` Owner: Shaq. Target: next sprint. - **Add canonical normalisation to the reconciliation job's matching logic as a defence-in-depth measure** — so that even if a new format variant slips past ingestion, reconciliation still matches correctly. `[MITIGATE]` Owner: Shaq. Target: next sprint. - **Promote the reconciliation job's unmatched-supplier-id WARNING to an alert** — any non-zero count of unmatched IDs in a reconciliation run should fire a notification to the on-call channel. `[DETECT]` Owner: Pippen. Target: 1 week. - **Add a webhook input schema validation layer** — reject or quarantine payloads that contain `supplier_id` values not matching the expected canonical format, with an alert to the integration team. `[DETECT]` Owner: Shaq. Target: next sprint. - **Establish a tech-debt review cadence for items carrying data-quality or financial risk** — any TD item tagged with `data-quality` or `financial-impact` that has been in the backlog for more than 1 sprint must be explicitly re-triaged, not silently rolled forward. `[PROCESS]` Owner: Coach K. Target: implement by end of current sprint. - **Request a change-notification clause from the webhook provider** — formally ask the provider to notify consumers when sub-providers are onboarded or when the format of any field may change. `[PREVENT]` Owner: Bird (domain/vendor relationship). Target: 2 weeks. ## 6. Preserving the Learning - **ADR: "Canonical Identifier Normalisation at System Boundaries"** — document the decision that all externally-sourced identifiers must be normalised to a canonical format at the point of ingestion, with the rationale drawn from this incident. This applies to `supplier_id` now and to any future external identifier field. Owner: Bird. Target: 1 week. - **Domain rule addition to Bird's spec**: "Any field used for cross-system matching or reconciliation must have an explicit canonical format defined in the domain model, and normalisation to that format must be enforced at the ingestion boundary — not deferred as optional." Owner: Bird. - **Operational checklist item for Pippen**: "When reviewing monitoring for any reconciliation or matching job, verify that match-failure conditions produce alerts, not just log lines. WARNING-level logs that are not wired to alerts do not count as monitoring." Owner: Pippen. - **Sprint planning checklist item for Coach K**: "When deprioritising a risk item, record the assumptions that justified the deprioritisation. Add a review trigger (e.g., 'revisit if webhook goes live' or 'revisit next sprint, non-negotiable') so the risk acceptance does not become permanent by default." Owner: Coach K.
